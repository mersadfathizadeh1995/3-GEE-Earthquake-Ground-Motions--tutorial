    üéØ Primary Goal of Geotech EQ Engineers

    Predict three things for seismic ground motions traveling from the fault source to the site of interest:

    1. Intensity

    How strong is the shaking? (Amplitude)

    2. Frequency Content

    What frequencies dominate?

    3. Duration

    How long does it last?

    What Parameters Affect These?

    • Earthquake Magnitude ‚Äî bigger magnitude = more energy
    • Distance from fault ‚Äî energy attenuates with distance
    • Site Conditions ‚Äî local near-surface geology (soil vs rock)
    • Fault type / tectonic setting ‚Äî strike-slip, reverse, normal
    • Travel Path Geology ‚Äî deep earth (Eastern vs Western rock)
    • Special Effects ‚Äî directivity, basin effects, topographic effects

    With every earthquake our database of recorded ground motions increases. Most strong ground motions are recorded with accelerometers. Small amplitude motions may be recorded using velocity meters (seismometers).

    Wave Arrivals & Distance Effects

    How Ground Motion Changes With Distance

    Anatomy of an Accelerogram

    A typical acceleration time history shows distinct wave arrivals:

    NoiseBefore P-wave arrival
    P-wave arrivalFirst, fastest, compressional
    S-wave arrivalSlower, shear wave, higher amplitude
    Surface wavesLower frequency, dominant at large distances
    PGAPeak Ground Acceleration (absolute max value)

    Surface waves are lower frequency ‚Üí easier to see in velocity or displacement records

    ‚ö†Ô∏è What Happens as Distance Increases?

    • Intensity: PGA decreases (attenuates) with distance
    • P & S waves: Separate more; at ~2√ó thickness of crust (~50 miles), surface waves amplitudes produce peak ground motions at larger distances
    • Frequency Content: High frequencies attenuate more because the earth acts as a low-pass filter ‚Äî as distance increases, high frequencies damp out
    • Duration: As distance increases, duration increases (waves spread out)

    1. Intensity (Amplitude)

    How We Measure Shaking Strength

    Recording Ground Motions

    • Measure motions in 3 orthogonal directions: Vertical, N-S, E-W
    • Can transform to fault-parallel and fault-normal components
    • Basic form: time histories (time vs. acceleration)
    • For strong ground motion: directly measure acceleration, then integrate to get velocity and displacement

    Horizontal vs. Vertical Motions

    • Engineers typically assume horizontal motions are more damaging than vertical
    • Vertical motions important for: rotating/sensitive equipment, certain structures
    • Factors of safety against gravity-induced static vertical forces usually provide adequate resistance
    Max Vert Accel ‚âà (2/3) √ó Max Horiz Accel (old rule of thumb ‚Äî not always true!)
    More on V/H Ratios
    • V/H ratios are used to estimate the vertical spectrum
    • Bozorgnia and Campbell (2004) is the standard for V/H ratios
    • ASCE 7-16 code addresses vertical ground motions
    • V/H can be very large (2-5) in certain frequency ranges if large horizontal ground motions are expected

    Intensity Parameters

    PGA ¬∑ PGV ¬∑ PGD ¬∑ Arias Intensity

    Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA)

    • Most commonly used ground motion parameter
    • Largest absolute value of horizontal acceleration
    • As PGA goes up, damage generally goes up
    • But it is dependent on building period

    Peak Ground Velocity (PGV)

    • More accurate at intermediate frequencies
    • Less sensitive to high frequencies
    • May be a better indicator of potential damage

    Peak Ground Displacement (PGD)

    • Associated with low-frequency content
    • Less common ‚Äî errors can occur during signal processing

    Signal Processing Steps

    Raw acceleration time histories must be processed for:

    • Instrument response: calibration factors; must account for building response if sensor is in a building
    • Ambient noise: filtering out background noise
    • Baseline correction:
      • Time domain: fit a function to the acceleration record and subtract
      • Frequency domain: apply high-pass filter above ~0.1 Hz

    2. Frequency Content

    Fourier Spectra & Response Spectra

    Fourier Amplitude Spectra (FAS)

    Decomposes the time history into its frequency components using the Fourier Transform.

    • Shows amplitude vs. frequency
    • Predominant Period (Tp): the period with the highest Fourier amplitude
    • Rock sites ‚Üí higher frequency content (shorter Tp)
    • Soil sites ‚Üí lower frequency content (longer Tp)

    Response Spectrum

    Plot of maximum response of a family of SDOF oscillators with varying natural periods, for a given damping ratio (usually 5%).

    • Input = the time-domain equivalent of the FAS
    • General earthquake frequencies: 0.2 ‚Äì 20 Hz
    • Shows which building periods will experience the most shaking

    Rock sites show higher Sa at short periods; soil sites show higher Sa at longer periods.

    3. Duration

    How Long Does Shaking Last?

    Why Duration Matters

    Number of cycles of loading affects:

    • Degradation of stiffness and strength in soils and structures
    • Pore pressure build-up ‚Üí liquefaction risk!
    Lower Amplitude + Longer Duration may produce MORE damage than Higher Amplitude + Shorter Duration

    Bracketed Duration

    Time between the first and last exceedance of a threshold acceleration (typically 0.05g).

    Gilroy #1 (Rock)9.8 sec
    Gilroy #2 (Soil)14.7 sec

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Problem: stations far from the source may never exceed the threshold, so duration = 0.

    Significant Duration

    Based on Arias Intensity. Defined as the time interval across which a specified amount of energy is dissipated.

    • D‚ÇÖ‚Çã‚Çá‚ÇÖ: Time interval between 5% and 75% of total Ia (commonly used for structural)
    • D‚ÇÖ‚Çã‚Çâ‚ÇÖ: Time interval between 5% and 95% of total Ia (commonly used for geotech)

    Read from the Husid Plot (Ia vs time).

    Near-Fault Effects

    Rupture Directivity (R < 10-20 km)

    Rupture Directivity Explained

    Fault rupture is like ripping a piece of cloth. The rupture can initiate and rip either or both directions. The fault rupture propagates at a velocity ‚âà Vs (~3 km/sec).

    Each rupture point initiates shear waves that radiate away from that point. What happens depends on the observer's position:

    ‚úÖ Forward Directivity

    Site is ahead of the rupture propagation direction.

    • All S-waves arrive at the same time
    • Produces a high-amplitude, short-duration velocity pulse
    • Large 2-sided velocity pulse
    • Increased low-frequency (long-period) content
    • Manifests primarily in the fault-normal direction (due to radiation pattern of S-wave)

    ‚¨ÖÔ∏è Backward Directivity

    Site is behind the rupture propagation direction.

    • S-waves arrive all spread out
    • Produces a low-amplitude, long-duration pulse
    • Lower long-period content

    When Does Forward Directivity Occur?

    Two conditions must be met simultaneously:

    1. The rupture front propagates toward the site
    2. The direction of slip on the fault is aligned with the site

    These conditions are readily met in strike-slip faulting (horizontal slip along strike). Also met in dip-slip faulting (reverse and normal faults) ‚Äî rupture direction and slip direction up the fault plane produce effects at sites near the surface exposure of the fault.

    1992 Landers Earthquake (Mw = 7.3) ‚Äî Real Example

    Lucerne station (forward directivity): MHA = 0.76g, 1 km from rupture

    Joshua Tree station (backward directivity): MHA = 0.284g

    The Lucerne record shows a large 2-sided velocity pulse with increased long-period content and short duration pulse, primarily in the fault-normal direction.

    Fling Step & Pulse Classification

    Permanent Displacement & Identifying Pulses

    Fling Step (R < 5 km)

    • Permanent ground displacement over a short time due to fault offset
    • Caused by a 1-sided velocity pulse
    • For strike-slip faults ‚Üí fling step is on the fault-parallel component
    • For dip-slip faults ‚Üí fling step is on the fault-normal component

    Important: You don't see permanent displacement in Sd (spectral displacement) because Sd is relative displacement.

    Pulse Classification ‚Äî Shahi and Baker (2014)

    It's not easy to classify a motion as pulse or non-pulse. Shahi and Baker (2014) use PGV to classify ground motions:

    If Pulse Indicator (PI) > 0 ‚Üí motion is classified as a PULSE

    Classifying motions as pulse vs non-pulse is important so that ground motion modeling can include directivity effects in the design ground motions.

    Topographic Effects

    How Hills & Ridges Amplify Ground Motion

    The Question

    Would all "Rock Sites" on a hillside experience the same ground motion during an earthquake, all other things being equal? Absolutely not!

    1994 Northridge Earthquake ‚Äî Tarzana Station

    PGA = 1.78g ‚Äî "one of the highest accelerations ever recorded" (Spudich et al. 1996)

    Hill height15 m
    Hill length500 m
    Hill width130 m

    21 three-component geophones deployed; 16 aftershocks analyzed:

    Transverse resonant freq~3.2 Hz
    Peak/Base amp ratio~4.5

    6 Rules of Topographic Amplification

    Key Findings from Research

    Rule 1

    Max amplification occurs at or near the peak of the ridge. Max deamplification occurs near the toe (base). Irregular patterns in between.

    Rule 2

    Amplification is related to "sharpness" of the topography: the steeper the average slope ‚Üí the higher the amplification.

    Rule 3

    Amplifications are highly frequency-dependent and occur at wavelengths (Œª) comparable to the feature's characteristic length:

    Œª ‚âà W (width)   or    Œª ‚âà 5 √ó H (height)
    V = f √ó Œª   ‚Üí   f = V / Œª

    (Paolucci 2002, Ashford et al. 1997)

    Rule 4

    Amplification in the direction perpendicular to the ridge elongation is larger than parallel.

    Rule 5

    The vertical direction is affected far less by topographic amplification.

    Rule 6

    Amplification factors from field observations are significantly higher than numerical simulations!

    Field StudiesAmp 3-6 common, up to 10+

    Spudich 1996, Massa 2010, Hough 2011, Beuch 2010

    Numerical SimsAmp rarely > 3, common < 2

    Paolucci 2002, Papadimitriou 2011

    Topographic Effects in Practice

    Codes, Formulas & Recommendations

    Eurocode 8 (Part 5, Annex A)

    For topography with H > 30 m, amplification factors (S_T) range from 1.2 ‚Äì 1.4.

    • Considered independent of fundamental period of vibration
    • A constant scaling factor applied to the elastic design response spectrum

    ‚ö†Ô∏è This is NOT a good approach because amplification is under-estimated in the hill resonant frequency range and over-estimated everywhere else.

    Professor Wood's Recommendations

    Use the Paolucci (2002) approach to estimate the topographic amplification frequency range:

    f = (0.7 to 1.0) √ó (Vs_avg / Length)    for ridge features

    Can also use Ashford et al. (1997):

    f_t0 = Vs_avg / (5.0 √ó Height)
    • Amplifications of 2.5 - 3.25 times flat ground response are typical
    • Isolated or peninsula-type features can have amplification of 10 times!

    Basin Effects

    Trapped Waves & Edge Effects

    How Basins Affect Ground Motions

    • Seismic waves (Rayleigh waves) can be trapped in the basin
    • This leads to longer duration and higher ground motions (constructive interference)
    • Significant amplifications occur near the edge of the basin as Rayleigh waves strike and bounce off (impedance difference)

    Current State of Knowledge

    We still don't have a good enough understanding to capture basin effects in edge and deep basins, but do OK with shallow basins.

    Mostly accounted for in GMPEs via:

    Z1.0

    Depth to Vs = 1.0 km/s

    Z2.5

    Depth to Vs = 2.5 km/s

    Vs30

    Avg shear wave vel, top 30m

    GMPEs: Your Homework Guide

    Attenuation Relationships for HW #3

    What is a GMPE?

    A Ground Motion Prediction Equation (formerly "attenuation relationship") is a mathematical model that predicts ground motion intensity (PGA, Sa, etc.) as a function of earthquake parameters.

    ln(Y) = f(Magnitude, Distance, Site, Fault Style, ...)

    The output is typically the median prediction. Adding ¬± œÉ (standard deviation) gives you the ¬± 1œÉ confidence bounds. For your HW, use only the median (ignore œÉ).

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: Distance Definitions

    Different GMPEs use different distance metrics! This is crucial for Question 1:

    Rupture Distance (R_rup)

    Closest distance to the fault rupture plane (in 3D)

    Used by: Abrahamson & Silva 1997, Idriss 2008

    Joyner-Boore Distance (R_JB)

    Shortest distance to the surface projection of the rupture

    Used by: Boore, Joyner & Fumal 1997

    ‚ö†Ô∏è For HW: you must estimate R_JB since only R_rup is given!
